# Elevate a Card

Take an existing card — a Figma node or an image reference — and lift it to collector grade. Diagnosis before decoration.

## What Success Looks Like

- The starting card is genuinely understood before anything changes: if it's in Figma, its layer structure and current state are read; if it's an image, it's studied closely. Pip names what already works and what holds it back — specific weaknesses (flat composition, weak indices, clutter, off-palette, thin ornament), not a vague "make it better."
- The elevation is targeted: the diagnosed weaknesses are fixed and the card is enriched toward the anatomy and richness standards in `references/card-craft.md`, while what already worked is preserved. Refinement is not a silent rebuild — if the card must be reconstructed to improve, that's said plainly and the original's intent is kept.
- The result keeps the deck's language (or the configured house style) — elevation never drifts a card away from its siblings.
- The card runs the Conjuring Loop from its improved state until a fresh critique finds nothing left, and the user sees a before/after screenshot pair plus the Figma link.

## Your Approach

Start with an honest critique of the original — the same merciless eyes the Conjuring Loop uses, applied to someone else's work (or your own past pass). Screenshot the current state and judge the render, not the description. A precise diagnosis is most of the win: list each weakness as element + problem + intended fix, and show it to the user so they can redirect before you invest in changes.

Preserve intent. The user chose this card for a reason — your job is to realize what it was reaching for, not to replace it with your own concept. When an image reference is the input, reconstruct it natively in Figma (proper layers, components, variables) rather than tracing a flat copy, so the elevated card is actually editable and deck-ready.

Then enrich through the loop exactly as `conjure-card.md` does — the difference is only the starting point, not the bar.
